Finding a GP in Ontario — Important
~2 million Ontario residents currently have no family doctor. This is a known crisis.
Register on Health Care Connect (ontario.ca/page/find-family-doctor) as soon as you have an Ontario address.
Use walk-in clinics freely in the meantime — covered by OHIP, no appointment needed.
Virtual GP services: Maple, Telus Health — often covered by employer benefits. Excellent for non-emergency needs.
Pharmacy clinics (Shoppers Drug Mart, Rexall) can handle many minor issues without a GP referral.

HOOPP — Healthcare of Ontario Pension Plan
Olivia's most important financial asset if she qualifies
What is HOOPP?
HOOPP is a defined benefit (DB) pension plan — one of the best in Canada. Unlike a TFSA or RRSP where your payout depends on market performance, HOOPP guarantees a specific income for life based on a formula. Markets go up or down — your HOOPP pension doesn't change. It is fully funded and one of the most stable pension plans in the world.
Who qualifies?
HOOPP covers employees at participating healthcare employers in Ontario. CAMH is a HOOPP employer — Olivia would almost certainly be enrolled automatically as a full-time registered nurse. Humber River Health is also a HOOPP employer. Part-time staff may need to opt in — check on day one of employment.
How the pension is calculated
Annual pension = 2% × years of service × best 5-year average salary
Example: 30 years service at avg CAD $85k = CAD $51,000/year for life, inflation-indexed. This is on top of TFSA and CPP — not instead of them.
Contributions
Employee contributes roughly 6.9–9% of salary depending on earnings. Employer (CAMH/Humber) matches contributions. Both come off payroll automatically. The more years Olivia works in Ontario healthcare, the larger her pension — every year counts significantly.
Portability
If Olivia moves between HOOPP employers (e.g. CAMH → Humber → Ontario Shores) her pension credits transfer seamlessly. If she leaves Ontario healthcare entirely, she can either take a deferred pension (paid at 65) or transfer the commuted value to an RRSP/LIRA — nothing is lost.
Why this matters so much
Most Canadians rely entirely on market-based savings (TFSA/RRSP) which can fall 30–40% in a bad year. Olivia having a guaranteed, inflation-protected income for life from HOOPP fundamentally changes your retirement picture. It means Tariq's TFSA is upside — not necessity. This is genuinely rare and valuable.
Action on day one at CAMH or Humber: Confirm HOOPP enrolment with HR. Make sure you are enrolled as a full member not just a deferred member. Ask for your member number and register at hoopp.com. Start your pension statement tracking from year one — every year of credited service adds approximately 2% of your best average salary per year, for life.

Taxes
Irish exit tax, Canadian first return & ongoing obligations for Tariq & Olivia
Year of Departure — Ireland
In the year you leave Ireland, you are tax resident for the part of the year you were there. You must file a Form 12 (or Form 11 if self-assessed) for that tax year. You may be entitled to a tax refund if you overpaid PAYE. Notify Revenue Ireland of your departure date — file a P50 if leaving employment mid-year.
First Year in Canada
Canada taxes on residency, not citizenship. You become a Canadian tax resident from the day you establish residential ties (apartment, SIN, bank account). Your first tax return (filed by 30 April the following year) covers only the Canadian-resident portion. You need to declare worldwide income from that date — including any Irish income earned after you became Canadian resident.
Double Taxation Treaty
Ireland and Canada have a Double Taxation Agreement (DTA) — you will not be taxed twice on the same income. If you pay tax in Ireland on income, Canada gives you a foreign tax credit. Critically, this also covers Tariq's Irish pension at age 60 — it will be taxed once, in the country of residence at the time of drawdown, not both.
Irish Pension — Key Tax Points
When Tariq draws down his Irish pension at 60 from Canada: the 25% tax-free lump sum under Irish rules may still apply, though the DTA determines which country taxes the remainder. Irish Life will withhold Irish tax unless a DTA exemption is claimed. At that point, get specialist cross-border tax advice — it will be worth it on a pot that could be €80–100k+. Keep all pension statements and contribution records permanently.

Neighbourhood Guide — Within Your Criteria
Etobicoke ★ Recommended
A large west-end district bordering the lake. Quieter and more suburban than the city core — good for two people settling into a new country without the overwhelm of downtown. Strong Irish and South Asian communities. Kipling and Islington subway stations give a direct Line 1 ride to Union and on to CAMH. Good mix of supermarkets, parks and local restaurants without the Toronto premium. Ikea, Costco and big-box stores easily accessible. Feels a bit like a large Dublin suburb — familiar energy.
North York
The largest borough in the old city, now a dense urban area in its own right. Yonge and Sheppard is a proper urban hub with towers, malls and excellent transit. Very multicultural — some of the best and most diverse food in the GTA. Yonge-University Line 1 runs straight to Union and Queen Street. Feels more like a city than Etobicoke — busier, more energy. North York Centre station area has everything you need within walking distance. Good if you want a more urban feel without paying downtown prices.
East End / Danforth
Known locally as "The Danforth" — a long stretch of restaurants, cafes and independent shops running east from the Don Valley. Historically a Greek neighbourhood, now very mixed. Has a real community feel that is unusual for a city Toronto's size. Pape, Donlands and Greenwood stations on Line 2 give a quick ride to Union and a straightforward connection to Queen Street for CAMH. The beach and Woodbine Park are nearby. Popular with young professional couples. Feels the most European of the options — reminiscent of a lively Dublin neighbourhood.
Leslieville / Riverside
One of Toronto's most popular areas for young professionals — dense with independent coffee shops, restaurants and a strong weekend market culture. Sits just east of the downtown core along Queen Street East. The 501 Queen streetcar runs directly to CAMH in around 20 minutes, making it arguably Olivia's best commute option. Very walkable and bikeable. The tradeoff is space — a 2-bed here at your budget would be smaller or require a stretch. Worth viewing a few places to compare the size difference versus the other areas.
Downtown Core — Skip
The area between Bloor and the lake, roughly Spadina to Jarvis. Extremely convenient for transit and amenities but you pay a significant premium for space that shrinks accordingly. A 2-bed here is rare at your budget and would typically mean a very dated building or a compromised location within downtown. The financial and lifestyle gains from living slightly further out and using the excellent TTC are hard to ignore — you lose very little in terms of access but gain meaningfully on space, quality of life and monthly savings that can go into your TFSA instead.
What to Check Before Signing — Ontario Lease
The unit
✓ Test all appliances, taps, heating and AC
✓ Check water pressure and hot water
✓ Look for mould, especially bathrooms
✓ Check window locks and exterior doors
✓ Photograph everything before moving in
✓ Confirm laundry — in-unit or shared?
✓ Check internet providers available (Rogers/Bell)
The lease
✓ Ontario Standard Lease Form only — be wary of anything else
✓ First + last month is the legal maximum deposit
✓ Landlord cannot ask for more than 2 months upfront
✓ Confirm what's included (heat, hydro, water, parking)
✓ Check rent increase rules — Ontario limits annual increases
✓ Get landlord's full legal name and contact in writing
✓ Never pay cash — e-transfer with written confirmation only
